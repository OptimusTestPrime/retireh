Assumptions & Methodology

This tool models your retirement by running a year-by-year simulation based on standard Canadian rules and your economic inputs.

Economic Logic

  • Growth & Inflation: These rates are specified by you. Your desired net income and expected government benefits are adjusted annually by the inflation rate to maintain your purchasing power over decades.

Government Benefits (CPP & OAS) Timing

  • Strategy: You can choose when to start your benefits. Delaying them can significantly increase your retirement income and minimize lifetime taxes.
  • CPP (Canada Pension Plan): Can be started between age 60 and 70. Starting early permanently reduces your pension by 7.2% per year before 65. Delaying increases it by 8.4% per year after 65. We assume an average base payout of ~$11,500/year (today's dollars) at age 65.
  • OAS (Old Age Security): Can be started between age 65 and 70. Delaying increases the payout by 7.2% per year after 65. We assume a full base payout of ~$9,100/year (today's dollars) at age 65.

Employer Pension Rules

  • Federal (PSSA): Applies a 5% penalty per year if you collect before age 60 (Group 1) or 65 (Group 2), unless you have 30 years of service. An estimated bridge benefit is paid until age 65.
  • Provincial (85 Factor): Applies a 3% penalty for every year you are short of reaching 85 points (Age + Years of Service) or age 65, whichever penalty is smaller.

Taxation & Withdrawal Strategy

Because you enter a Net (After-Tax) income goal, the engine must calculate the "Gross" amount you need to withdraw to pay the CRA and still hit your target. It does this using a blended tax model:

  • Account Treatment:
    • Pensions, CPP, OAS, and RRSP/RRIF withdrawals: Treated as 100% taxable regular income.
    • TFSA withdrawals: Treated as 100% tax-free.
    • Non-Registered withdrawals: Approximated as 25% taxable (assuming the withdrawal is half tax-free principal and half capital gains at a 50% inclusion rate).
  • Tax Brackets (Blended Fed/Prov): The model uses an estimated combined tax profile. It applies a tax-free Basic Personal Amount (~$15,500), then applies a 22% tax rate on the next $50,000 of taxable income, and a 38% rate on income above that. Crucially, these brackets are indexed to your inflation rate yearly to prevent artificial "bracket creep."
  • The "Gross-Up" Loop: To minimize your taxes, the calculator draws income in a specific, tax-efficient order: 1) Guaranteed Income (Pensions/CPP/OAS), 2) Non-Registered, 3) RRSP, and 4) TFSA. If your guaranteed income falls short of your net goal, the engine iteratively withdraws from your savings, calculating the new tax bill at each step, until your take-home pay matches your desired target exactly.
